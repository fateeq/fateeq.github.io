Gaining Insights From Product Data

A data analysis project completed for work

Last year, I worked as a Regulatory Affairs Specialist for a veterinary pharmaceutical company. As part of a project, the manager ordered eight animal feeds to be manufactured at various feed mills. Our lab performed the chemical analysis on the feeds and reported the results on an Excel spreadsheet, with any non-conforming data highlighted in red. The manager handed over the spreadsheet to me - which consisted of 600 data points, half of which were non-conforming. He asked me to make the best of a bad situation, mentioning that this was the third time he had the feeds manufactured due to half the data not conforming.

I discovered that there were several major factors contributing to the bad data, including errors in manufacturing, sampling, lab analysis, malfunctioning equipment, incorrect spreadsheet calculations, and even doing the wrong statistics. I learnt about the feed manufacturing process from manuals, looked through manufacturing batch record for errors, talked with lab analysts through the entire process step-by-step of how they collected samples and prepared their chemical solutions, asked the manager how the feed gets used in real life and how best to look at the data, and also consulted a statistician on the calculations that were being performed.

Based on my new insights, I was able to clean up the data and conducted the data analysis using proper statistical methods. This resulted in only 3-4 non-conforming data points, and I was able to give an explanation for each of those remaining non-conforming points. This helped my team move on to the next step of the project. It also saved this project from failing FDA's approval and saved the team $50K as well as 6 months of project time due to not having to manufacture the feeds a fourth time.

Tools used: MS Excel

Comparison of Two Different Tools

A data cleansing, analysis and visualization project

This project was one of many that I completed as part of my M.Sc. thesis.
(My thesis was on the removal of phosphorus from wastewater to low levels and the chemical analysis of phosphorus. Link to abstract)

Question to be answered: is there a correlation between Method 1 (ICP-OES) and Method 2 (colorimetry with persulfate digestion)?

Dataset: Measurement results of phosphorus concentration (reported in units of mg P/L) from two different chemical analytical methods (ICP-OES and colorimetry with persulfate digestion) on 91 wastewater samples from various sources. Some samples had duplicate and triplicate measurements. The 91 samples were further divided into three groups of 6, 18 and 67 depending on specific type and source of the wastewater sample.

Steps:

  1. Use averages and standard deviations to represent duplicate and triplicate measurements.
  2. Remove unusable data (for example, anything below detection limit of the analytical instruments).
  3. Merge data from various spreadsheets into one and format to visualize data easily.
  4. Perform exploratory calculations and analyses and note down all insights.
  5. Create final well-formatted visualizations to show concepts.

Insights:

  1. As the slope and the intercept are higher than 1 and 0 respectively in Figure 3, the linear regression analysis implies that the TP measured by ICP is consistently higher than that measured by colorimetry.
  2. The relationship of ICP-P/colorimetry-P to colorimetry-P (shown in Figure 4) indicates that the deviation from the 1:1 is increasingly more significant for concentrations below 0.15 mg P/L.
